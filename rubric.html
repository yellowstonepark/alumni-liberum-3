The Refugee experience connects to both the novel “Inside Out and Back Again” and the article “Children of War” because both begin with the universal journey of the fleeing of a war and end with a rigorous journey to finding a new home and settling there. Although the journey’s differences may be there, they all struggle with similar challenges to overcome. The lives of refugees turn inside out and back again when they are forced to flee and find a new home. In the beginning, their experience is dominated by the feeling that they are losing their rights or ability to be in a country they once called home. While they miss some of the good, familiar things they left there, they realize that their home country is unsafe. Then, they have to go through a journey to find a new home and be safe once again and be in a better position than before. In “Inside Out and Back Again,” Ha’s life is turned inside out when she is forced to flee her home. Ha was once used to an environment she considered stable. The tear up of her country and finding a new home are obviously all new to her and very tough challenges to overcome. When Ha finds her new home her life has turned back again because she is in a secure place with a home and getting an education which is very similar to her situation at the beginning of the novel of how she is in a tight net of security. The novel focuses on a place to live, food, and generally most of the essential necessities of life and how Ha loses that sense of security through her journey and then, once in a similar situation, begins to regain it and, in this way, her new life starts to be similar to her past life. The novel shows that refugee experiences come with many negative consequences but can help you not only come back to a sense of normality but also a better life than ever before with better opportunities. The article “Children of War”“ starts by explaining that “Since the war began two years ago, more than 200,000 people have been killed, while another 2 million have been driven from their homes. As in most wars, young people suffered their share, even though they didn't start the war and are too young to fight in it. A recent Harvard study estimates that 30,000 children have been killed. Tens of thousands more have been orphaned. And nearly 25 percent of all the refugees created by the war are between the ages of 10 and 17. Although all ethnic groups in Bosnia have been affected by the war, The hardest hit have been Muslims. Today, tens of thousands ofyoung Muslim war victims are languishing in refugee camps in Croatia, hoping eventually to make it to safety in another country. Last year, the U.S. admitted 3,000 of these refugees.” This piece of evidence shows that the lives of young people have been turned inside out as many were killed or driven from their homes and forced into refugee camps in Croatia, waiting for a new home which may provide safety. The interview with the teenagers shows how they remember the good points of their past life. One of them is quoted as saying “Yes, before the war, life was good. My father had a good job and we had lots of money. Every year we would travel to foreign countries. We would go to Russia, Bulgaria, Hungary - all over.” Another teenager named Elma said “It seemed like we had no worries. I had lots of friends and we would all go skiing in the mountains. It was safe in Bosnia in those days. Bosnia was a wonderful place to live.” The interviewer asked them “How did the war change your lives?” A teenager named Emir replied “After the war started, you could not even go out of your house. I had to crawl through my apartment on my hands and knees or risk getting shot.” This evidence shows that the teenagers had a good life before the war with many friends and nice trips. However, after the war, people were trapped in their houses and had to crawl on their hands and knees to avoid getting shot. In both the “Children of War” article and the novel, the young people are very influenced by the tragic lives of their parents and relatives and the politics of the countries they had left that are both quite different in their new homes.. In the article, a teenager named Amelia said “Before the war I really enjoyed life. But after I found out about my father's death, everything seemed so useless. I couldn't see any future for myself. I didn't know where I was going. I wasn't the same person anymore. The interviewer asked, “What are your lives like in the U.S.?” Amela replied “I like it better than being a refugee in Croatia. Here, people don't judge you by your religion. When I say that I'm a Muslim, they don't react like, ‘Oh, I don't want to be with you, I don't want to be your friend because you're Muslim.’ Some people here don't even know where Bosnia is, but they're really nice and try to help. Things are getting better because we can go to school. We couldn't go to school in Croatia because we are Muslims.” This piece of evidence shows that the teenagers’ lives were back again in the US where nobody is getting killed and they were able to go to school and not be discriminated for being Muslim. Refugees' lives turn inside out and back again through the settling of a home to having to move because of the consequences of a war to starting to find a new home and adapt to it. An example of how Ha is influenced by her family’s conditions in the novel is: “My best friend TiTi is crying hard,... People can barely afford food.... .” The quote shows that Ha’s family is very different than what it used to be and all of her friends and family have been negatively affected by the Vietnam War. She also says that no one can afford anything which makes Ha’s mom try to savour everything she owns to the maximum extent. She stated “Hardly anyone buys anymore, she says. People can barely afford food.... .” Another example is on page 38 where it states, "A siren screams over Miss Xinh's voice in the middle of a lesson on smiley and bald president fort. We all know its bad news. Schools now closed; everyone must go home a month too soon." The quote explains that something dangerous probably happened leading to the closing of schools. She also says that everyone knows that it is bad news and, based on past poems talking about the Vietnam War, we can infer it is a consequence of the war. Had she lived in another time period or country this would not have happened. All in all, Ha’s life has been greatly affected by where she lives and what is happening to her family. This is evident because of her family’s situation changing for the worse with no happiness and barely any money to support themselves. She is influenced by a missing father and by the loss of one of the people that made her smile, her best friend who moved away because of the war. Her family and friends' situation changed greatly as they go through their journey. The novel describes how Ha’s life is turns back again once she finds a new home. The novel says: “Mother invites our cowboy and MiSSSisss WaSShington for egg rolls. They brought gifts, not saying Early Christmas,not wanting to embarrass us for not having anything to exchange. Although Ha and her family did not think this at first but the family’s neighbors at her new home were very nice. They even bought gifts for them showing that the neighbors care about Ha and her family. Also, the family thought that the gifts were perfect so that must mean the neighbors know them well. Ha’s life is back again when she describes her house. This is seen in the quote: “I inspect our house. Two bedrooms, one for my brothers, one for Mother and me. The two texts closely relate and show the true way refugees turn inside out at one point and back again at another.

Rubrics for Common Core English Language Arts Tests, excerpted from the Guide

New English Language Arts Rubrics

The Common Core English Language Arts Test will be scored using new rubrics. Both

the English Language Arts 2-Point and 4-Point Rubrics have changed to reflect the

new demands called for by the CCLS.

Short-Response (2-Point) Holistic Rubric

Short-response questions will ask students to make a claim, take a position, or draw a

conclusion, and then support it with details. This structure forms the foundation of the

CCLS. As such, the 2- point Rubric focuses on both the inference and evidence a student

provides. This structure allows students to have wide latitude in responding to each

prompt so long as their response is supported by the text.

Additionally, the expectation for all short responses will be complete, coherent

sentences. By weaving these elements together, the questions, responses, and scores

remain firmly focused on student reading ability.

2-Point Rubric—Short Response

Score Response Features

2 Point The features of a 2-point response are

 Valid inferences and/or claims from the text where required

by the prompt

 Evidence of analysis of the text where required by the prompt

 Relevant facts, definitions, concrete details, and/or other

information from the text to develop response according to the

requirements of the prompt

 Sufficient number of facts, definitions, concrete details, and/or

other information from the text as required by the prompt

 Complete sentences where errors do not impact readability

1 Point The features of a 1-point response are

 A mostly literal recounting of events or details from the text as

required by the prompt

 Some relevant facts, definitions, concrete details, and/or

other information from the text to develop response

according to the requirements of the prompt

 Incomplete sentences or bullets

0 Point* The features of a 0-point response are

 A response that does not address any of the requirements of the

prompt or is totally inaccurate

 A response that is not written in English

 A response that is unintelligible or indecipherable

 If the prompt requires two texts and the student only references one text, the response can be

scored no higher than a 1.

* Condition Code A is applied whenever a student who is present for a test session leaves an entire

constructed- response question in that session completely blank (no response attempted).

Extended-Response (4-Point) Holistic Rubric

Within the Common Core, writing does not take place in a vacuum. To be college and

career ready, one must be able to write for a purpose using information from textual

sources. Extended-response questions on the Common Core English Language Arts

Tests will ask students to analyze texts and address meaningful questions using strategic,

textual details. Scores for extended responses will be based on four overarching criteria:

 Content and Analysis—the extent to which the essay conveys complex ideas and

information clearly and accurately in order to support claims in an analysis of topics or

texts

 Command of Evidence—the extent to which the essay presents evidence from the

provided texts to support analysis and reflection

 Coherence, Organization, and Style—the extent to which the essay logically

organizes complex ideas, concepts, and information using formal style and precise

language

 Control of Conventions—the extent to which the essay demonstrates command

of the conventions of standard English grammar, usage, capitalization, punctuation,

and spelling

These four characteristics combined make up the focus of the 4-point, extended- response tasks, Writing from Sources. Whether in response to an individual text or a

paired selection, a student will be asked to synthesize, evaluate, and evidence his or her

thinking in a coherent and legible manner. Please note the holistic 4-point rubric for

Writing in Grades 6-8 that follows.
